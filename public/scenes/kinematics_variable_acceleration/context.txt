Theory:
Variable acceleration occurs when the rate of change of velocity is not constant over time. In contrast to uniformly accelerated motion (where a = constant), variable acceleration requires calculus to determine the object's state at any given moment.

Explanation:
When acceleration a(t) is a function of time:
1. Velocity v(t) is the integral of acceleration: v(t) = v₀ + ∫ a(t) dt
2. Position x(t) is the integral of velocity: x(t) = x₀ + ∫ v(t) dt

In this simulation, you can observe:
- A rocket: As it burns fuel, its mass decreases and its thrust may change, leading to variable acceleration (demonstrated here by manually adjusting thrust).
- An elevator: Starting from rest, an elevator must accelerate smoothly (variable acceleration) until it reaches a constant speed, then decelerate (variable acceleration) to stop.

Facts:
- The slope of a velocity-time graph represents the instantaneous acceleration.
- The area under an acceleration-time graph represents the change in velocity.
- Real-world motion is almost always variable acceleration due to changing forces like friction, air resistance, and engine output.

Key Equations:
- a = dv/dt (instantaneous acceleration)
- v = dx/dt (instantaneous velocity)
- Δv = ∫ a(t) dt
- Δx = ∫ v(t) dt
