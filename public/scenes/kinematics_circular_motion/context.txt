Theory:
Circular motion is a movement of an object along the circumference of a circle or rotation along a circular path. It can be uniform (constant angular rate) or non-uniform (changing angular rate).

Explanation:
Even when an object moves at a constant speed in a circle, it is accelerating because its direction is constantly changing. This acceleration is always directed toward the center of the circle.

Key Concepts:
1. Angular Velocity (ω): The rate of change of angular displacement (measured in radians/second).
2. Tangential Velocity (v): The linear speed of the object, v = rω. It is always tangent to the circle.
3. Centripetal Acceleration (a_c): The center-directed acceleration, a_c = v²/r = rω².

In this simulation:
- Two masses are placed on a rotating disk at different radii. 
- Although they have the same angular velocity (they complete one revolution in the same time), the outer mass has a much higher tangential velocity because it travels a larger circumference.

Facts:
- Centripetal force is not a "new" force but a requirement; it can be provided by tension, friction, gravity, etc.
- "Centrifugal force" is a fictitious force perceived only in a rotating (non-inertial) frame of reference.

Key Equations:
- v = 2πr / T
- ω = Δθ / Δt
- a_c = v² / r = ω²r
- F_c = ma_c = mv² / r
