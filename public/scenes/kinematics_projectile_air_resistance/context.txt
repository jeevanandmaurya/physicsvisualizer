Theory:
Air resistance (drag) is a force acting opposite to the relative motion of any object moving with respect to a surrounding fluid (like air). Unlike gravity, which is constant, drag depends on the object's speed.

Explanation:
At moderate to high speeds, the drag force F_d is approximately proportional to the square of the object's velocity:
F_d = ½ ρ v² C_d A
Where:
- ρ is the density of the fluid (air)
- v is the speed of the object
- C_d is the drag coefficient (shape dependent)
- A is the cross-sectional area

In this simulation:
- Blue Ball: Moves in a vacuum (F_d = 0). It follows a perfect parabola.
- Red Ball: Experiences air resistance. Notice how its trajectory is no longer symmetric; it falls more steeply than it rose.

Facts:
- Drag depends on the square of velocity, meaning if you double your speed, the drag quadruples.
- Terminal velocity is reached when the downward force of gravity equals the upward force of drag (a = 0).
- For projectiles with drag, the launch angle for maximum range is typically less than 45°.

Key Equations:
- F_net = mg + F_drag
- Terminal Velocity: v_t = √((2mg) / (ρAC_d))
- Range (with drag) < v₀²sin(2θ)/g
